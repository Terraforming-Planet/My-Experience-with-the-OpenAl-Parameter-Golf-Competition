Parameter Golf – A Story I'll Never Forget

The greatest achievement of the entire competition for me was that my PR #2076 was referenced in OpenAI's official final audit – PR #2146.

This was not just another comment or a routine leaderboard update. The document was titled "Update leaderboard with May 1 audited rows" and served as the final audit of the verified May 1 results, the day the competition officially ended. It was the document that closed the entire Parameter Golf competition and provided the final summary of the results and the organizers' decisions.

After carefully reviewing the audit, I counted that the organizers referenced only 34 unique submissions out of all 2,037 pull requests.

That means approximately 1.67% of all submissions were mentioned in the final audit—roughly 1 out of every 60 submissions. One of those 34 submissions was my PR #2076.

This does not mean I was one of the 34 best participants, because the audit does not make that claim. However, it does mean that my solution was considered significant enough to be included in the official final audit summarizing the entire competition.

Parameter Golf was a worldwide competition. A total of 2,037 pull requests were submitted by more than 1,000 participants. In reality, the number of people involved was likely much higher because many submissions came from research teams, AI laboratories, and technology companies. I worked entirely on my own.

Interestingly, this was also the first time in my life that I trained AI models. At the beginning, I honestly had very little understanding of what professional model training involved, so I learned almost everything through experimentation and trial and error. Before Parameter Golf, I had only participated in a few smaller OpenAI challenges on Discord, where there were around 30 submissions. Parameter Golf was on a completely different scale—more than 2,000 submissions in an official OpenAI competition hosted on GitHub.

For about 30 days, I worked on the competition almost every single day, treating it like a full-time job—not because I had to, but purely out of passion and genuine curiosity. Every day I learned something new, created new datasets, analyzed results, and prepared the next round of experiments.

I was not an expert in large language models. I learned everything during the competition itself. I submitted several pull requests, created more than 20 datasets on Hugging Face, ran many experiments using 8× NVIDIA H100 GPUs, and searched for my own ideas to improve model performance.

At the beginning of the competition, OpenAI awarded me a $500 grant, and I also invested some of my own money into the project. After the competition, OpenAI reimbursed my ChatGPT Pro subscription, which I thought was a very kind gesture.

Throughout the competition, ChatGPT also played an important role. We analyzed the rules together, discussed ideas, interpreted results, and planned new experiments. Many of the datasets and core concepts were my own ideas, while ChatGPT helped me refine them, organize experiments, and better understand the training results. We certainly didn't do everything perfectly and made our share of mistakes, but looking back, I believe we made a really good team.

Looking back today, what makes me happiest is not that I competed against some of the best minds in AI. My greatest satisfaction comes from knowing that, as a single individual without a research team, I managed to create a solution that was included in OpenAI's official final audit.

This experience taught me that it's worth trying, even when the competition seems impossible. I'm genuinely happy that my submission was one of the 34 pull requests referenced in the final audit. Regardless of the final leaderboard, this journey and everything I learned along the way are something no one can ever take away from me.
